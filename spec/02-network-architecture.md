# Network Architecture

Seed Infrastructure Protocol  
Version: 0.1-draft  
Status: Experimental

---

## Overview

The Seed Infrastructure Protocol supports a **polycentric network architecture**
for coordinating seed stewardship across bioregions.

Rather than relying on centralized control, the system connects
local stewards, guild nodes, governance councils, and software agents
into a distributed ecological coordination network.

This architecture mirrors the structure of many living systems:

- decentralized
- adaptive
- resilient
- cooperative

---

## Architectural Layers

The network operates across several interconnected layers.

### Ecological Layer

The ecological layer consists of the living systems themselves.

Examples include:

- seed varieties
- soil ecosystems
- pollinator networks
- climate patterns
- biodiversity habitats

This layer represents the **living foundation** of the protocol.

---

### Steward Layer

Seed stewards are the primary participants in the network.

Stewards may include:

- gardeners
- farmers
- seed savers
- researchers
- community seed libraries
- ecological restoration groups

Stewards generate **stewardship events** such as:

- saving seed
- sharing seed
- documenting adaptation
- restoring habitat
- teaching others

These events form the basis of **Proof of Stewardship** records.

---

### Guild Node Layer

Guild nodes coordinate activity within a region or community.

Examples of guild nodes:

- local seed guilds
- community seed banks
- seed libraries
- regional agricultural networks
- educational institutions

Guild nodes help:

- validate stewardship events
- coordinate exchanges
- maintain regional seed diversity
- support new stewards

Guild nodes represent **local coordination hubs** rather than
central authorities.

---

### Governance Layer

Governance bodies help guide the evolution of the commons.

Possible governance structures include:

- councils selected through fractal sortition
- rotating stewardship committees
- guild assemblies
- regional coordination councils

Governance functions may include:

- resolving disputes
- recognizing stewardship contributions
- evolving protocol rules
- coordinating large-scale initiatives

Governance should remain **polycentric and participatory**.

---

### Agent Layer

Software agents assist the network by automating tasks
and supporting coordination.

Agents may help with:

- validating event structures
- linking stewardship records to seed accessions
- summarizing ecological contributions
- detecting data inconsistencies
- identifying patterns across regions

Agents support the network but do not replace
human stewardship and governance.

---

### Commons Ledger Layer

The commons ledger records stewardship events
and Proof of Stewardship records.

This ledger may exist as:

- distributed databases
- federated data stores
- decentralized ledgers
- replicated archival systems

The ledger records:

- stewardship events
- validation levels
- stewardship contributions
- ecological observations
- knowledge documentation

The ledger does **not represent ownership or financial assets**.
Instead, it records ongoing contributions to the commons.

---

## Information Flow

The network operates through a continuous flow of stewardship information.

Example flow:

Seed Stewards  
→ record stewardship events  
→ guild nodes assist validation  
→ agents process and organize records  
→ Proof of Stewardship entries generated  
→ commons ledger updated  
→ governance and coordination signals produced

This process allows the network to remain **adaptive and transparent**.

---

## Polycentric Design

The protocol intentionally avoids single points of control.

Key characteristics include:

- multiple guild nodes
- distributed stewardship
- federated knowledge sharing
- independent but cooperating regions

This approach improves resilience and prevents
concentration of authority.

---

## Federation Across Bioregions

Guild nodes and councils may federate across bioregions.

This federation enables:

- seed exchange across climates
- biodiversity preservation networks
- collaborative breeding projects
- coordinated ecological restoration
- knowledge sharing across cultures

Federation allows the network to grow without
losing regional autonomy.
