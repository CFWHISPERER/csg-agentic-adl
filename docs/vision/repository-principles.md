# Repository Principles

## Guiding practices for the Cascadia Seed Guild knowledge commons

The Cascadia Seed Guild repository is intended to function as a **Living Ecological AI Laboratory**.  
These principles guide how the repository is used, how contributions are made, and how humans and AI agents interact within it.

They are meant to preserve integrity, humility, and usefulness as the system grows.

These principles should be read alongside:

- `docs/vision/living-ecological-ai-lab.md`
- `docs/vision/agentic-stewardship.md`
- `docs/vision/seed-guild-node.md`

---

# 1. Life-Centered Orientation

The repository exists to support the continuity of living systems.

Seeds, soils, fungi, pollinators, plants, animals, and human communities all participate in the ecological relationships that sustain life.

Therefore:

- Human benefit alone is not the sole measure of value.
- Long-term ecological resilience takes precedence over short-term convenience.
- Contributions should respect the integrity of living systems.

Human beings are participants in these systems, not external managers of them.

---

# 2. Stewardship Before Control

The repository favors stewardship rather than domination.

This means prioritizing:

- observation before intervention
- learning before optimization
- diversity before uniformity
- local adaptation before centralized prescription

Technical tools should support caretaking and understanding rather than impose rigid abstractions onto living systems.

---

# 3. Human Responsibility Remains Central

Artificial intelligence tools may assist the repository, but **responsibility remains human**.

AI agents may:

- propose
- summarize
- organize
- suggest patterns

Human stewards must:

- review
- interpret
- approve
- take responsibility for published knowledge

A simple rule governs AI participation:

> agents may propose  
> humans steward

---

# 4. Evidence and Observation Matter

The repository values **real-world observation**.

Contributions should clearly distinguish between:

- observation
- interpretation
- speculation
- simulation

Whenever possible:

- cite sources
- record location and context
- document time and conditions
- link observations to species or ecological relationships

Field experience is welcomed alongside scientific literature.

Both forms of knowledge are valuable.

---

# 5. Ecological Relationships Are Primary

The repository should emphasize **relationships**, not isolated facts.

Seeds exist within networks of:

- soil organisms
- fungi
- pollinators
- climate conditions
- human stewardship
- neighboring plant species

Documentation should strive to reveal these relationships whenever possible.

Understanding connections is often more important than cataloging individual elements.

---

# 6. Diversity Strengthens Resilience

Biological diversity strengthens ecosystems.

Intellectual and cultural diversity can strengthen knowledge systems as well.

The repository should welcome contributions that reflect:

- different growing environments
- different farming traditions
- indigenous knowledge where shared with permission
- varied stewardship practices
- multiple ecological perspectives

Standardization may assist interoperability, but it must not erase useful diversity.

---

# 7. Transparency of Uncertainty

Ecological systems are complex.

The repository should not present speculative knowledge as settled fact.

When uncertainty exists:

- mark it clearly
- explain assumptions
- record conflicting observations if necessary

Honest uncertainty strengthens credibility.

---

# 8. Version History as Ecological Memory

Version history in the repository functions as a form of **knowledge lineage**.

It preserves:

- changes in understanding
- evolving practices
- historical observations
- corrections and refinements

Contributors should avoid rewriting history without explanation.

Clear commit messages and pull request descriptions help maintain a usable memory of how knowledge evolves.

---

# 9. Local Context Matters

Ecological knowledge is often **place-specific**.

Practices that succeed in one region may not succeed elsewhere.

Whenever possible, contributions should include context such as:

- region
- climate
- soil type
- water conditions
- altitude
- growing season

The Cascadia Seed Guild is rooted in the **Cascadia bioregion**, but knowledge from other regions may still provide valuable comparison.

---

# 10. Open Knowledge with Responsible Sharing

The repository is intended as a **knowledge commons**.

Knowledge about seeds, ecological relationships, and stewardship practices should remain accessible where possible.

However, responsible sharing also requires care:

- avoid exposing sensitive ecological locations
- respect indigenous knowledge sovereignty
- credit sources and contributors
- preserve provenance of seed lines when known

Openness should strengthen stewardship rather than enable exploitation.

---

# 11. Incremental Growth Over Grand Design

The repository should grow **organically and iteratively**.

Instead of attempting perfect architecture at the beginning:

- begin with simple schemas
- refine based on experience
- adapt structure as knowledge grows
- remain open to revision

Like ecological systems, the repository should evolve through feedback rather than rigid planning.

---

# 12. AI as Tool, Not Authority

AI tools may assist with:

- summarization
- structuring notes
- identifying patterns
- linking related knowledge
- drafting documentation

But AI should never be treated as:

- an ecological authority
- a governance authority
- a replacement for field observation
- a substitute for stewardship judgment

AI should reduce friction in knowledge work, not replace human responsibility.

---

# 13. Respect for Seed Lineage

Seeds carry genetic and cultural history.

Whenever possible, the repository should preserve:

- origin stories
- seed sources
- year of saving
- regional adaptations
- seed stewards involved

Maintaining lineage helps preserve both biological diversity and cultural continuity.

---

# 14. Humility Toward Living Systems

Living systems existed long before modern institutions and technologies.

They will likely persist long after them.

Therefore, the repository should operate with humility.

No model, dataset, or algorithm can fully capture ecological complexity.

The goal is not to dominate nature with better tools.

The goal is to **participate more intelligently and responsibly in the systems that sustain life.**

---

# 15. A Guild, Not a Platform

This repository supports a **guild of stewards**, not a centralized platform.

Guilds emphasize:

- shared learning
- responsibility
- craftsmanship
- trust
- continuity across generations

Technology should support those values rather than replace them.

---

# Closing Principle

The Cascadia Seed Guild repository exists to help cultivate a form of intelligence that is:

- ecological
- distributed
- humble
- practical
- stewarded

If the repository remains faithful to these principles, it may become a durable tool for supporting seed stewardship, ecological understanding, and bioregional resilience.
